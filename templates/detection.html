Detection Methods

How We Identify Microplastics in Food & Environment

1. Fourier-Transform Infrared Spectroscopy (FTIR)

FTIR spectroscopy identifies microplastics by measuring the specific infrared absorbance patterns of polymer bonds. This method can detect particles down to ~10 µm and distinguish between polymer types (e.g., polyethylene vs. polystyrene).

In practice, environmental samples (water, soil, food digests) are filtered and the retained particles analysed with an FTIR microscope, providing both qualitative and quantitative data on microplastic composition.

2. Raman Spectroscopy

Raman spectroscopy uses laser light scattering to produce a molecular fingerprint of microplastic particles. It’s capable of sub-micron resolution, making it ideal for detecting very small fragments in complex matrices.

After isolating particles, researchers target individual pieces with a focused laser, collecting Raman shifts that match known polymer reference spectra.

3. Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) & EDX

SEM provides high-resolution imaging of microplastic morphology, including surface roughness and particle shape. When coupled with Energy-Dispersive X-Ray spectroscopy (EDX), it confirms elemental composition, differentiating plastics from natural particulates.

Samples are coated with a thin conductive layer (e.g., gold) before imaging, allowing detailed visual and elemental mapping critical for forensic-style microplastic analysis.

4. Thermal Desorption–GC–MS (TD-GC-MS)

TD-GC-MS thermally degrades microplastics into monomeric units, which are then separated by gas chromatography and identified via mass spectrometry. This provides both polymer type and additive profiling.

It excels at quantifying low-abundance plastics in complex food samples, offering high sensitivity for trace microplastic detection.
